Superimposition Concept

What is superimposition?

Playing a scale or arpeggio rooted a half-step, whole-step, or tritone away from the chord root. The ear hears the clash against the tonal center, creating tension that resolves back home.

The "In and Out" Approach

Play 4 bars in → 2 bars out → 2 bars in. This is the Coltrane/McLaughlin method: establish the center firmly before displacing it. The key is always knowing where home is, and when to return.


Tip Target chord tones (3rd, 7th) on beat 1 and 3 of each measure even when playing "out" — the line sounds intentional, not lost.

Practice Regimen — Outside Playing

4-Week Outside Lines Plan

Week 1 (60 BPM): Inside only. Lock in the pentatonic. No outside notes. Know where every root and 3rd is.

Week 2 (70 BPM): Add ♭5 chromatic passing tones only. One outside note per phrase. Return to root.

Week 3 (80 BPM): Tritone arpeggio substitution. 2 beats out, resolve in. Target 3rd on the downbeat after.

Week 4 (100+ BPM): Full outside superimposition. 2-4 bars outside. Confident resolution. Add vibrato and bends to sell the line.

Subdivision Clarity

Outside lines sound intentional only when your subdivisions are locked. Use the metronome on beats 2&4 (backbeats) to internalize the jazz feel. 8th-note lines over a vamp at 80 BPM give you plenty of space for 4-note outside phrases.
